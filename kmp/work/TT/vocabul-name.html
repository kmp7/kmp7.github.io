Linguistic assessment of alternative terms for token vocabulary
About the Application

This application is designed for linguistics students and future foreign language teachers studying the terminology of modern language models. You will analyze the term Token vocabulary and propose your own alternative name that more accurately reflects the nature of this concept.

Why is this important? The term "vocabulary" (token vocabulary) has become historically established, but its structure does not coincide with the linguistic "vocabulary" (lexicon): tokens include bytes, subwords, frequent phrases, and special markers, and not just lexemes with semantics. Vocabulary formation is determined by algorithmic and statistical goals (encoding/compression, corpus coverage), not by linguistic categorization.

Context

In NLP, a token vocabulary is a finite set of units into which the tokenizer segments text. Unlike a linguistic vocabulary, this set may contain:

B
Individual bytes and characters
Sw
Subword units
Ph
Frequent phrases and fragments
Sp
Special markers (BOS, EOS, PAD)

The formation of this set is determined by statistical algorithms (BPE, WordPiece, Unigram), and not by linguistic analysis. Therefore, the term "vocabulary" may mislead linguists regarding the content and level of units.

Existing Alternative Terms

Below are alternative names that have been proposed to replace token vocabulary. Each term has its advantages and disadvantages.

token set / token inventory
Neutral, emphasizes the set nature of units without implying a linguistic dictionary.
encoding vocabulary
Points to the encoding function, but retains the ambiguous word "vocabulary."
token lexicon
Direct analogy with the linguistic term. Requires a caveat that it is not a classical lexicon.
segmentation inventory
Focuses on the segmentation process, but is somewhat verbose.
subword inventory
Accurate for subword tokenizers, but does not cover byte-level and whole-word approaches.
codebook / token codebook
Borrowed from information theory. Emphasizes encoding, but less familiar to linguists.
lexical codebook
Combines linguistic and informational aspects. Can cause confusion between levels.
unit inventory
Maximally neutral. Does not specify what kind of units are involved.
statistical token set
Explicitly indicates the statistical nature of formation. Slightly lengthy.
tokenizer lexicon
Links to the tokenizer tool, but retains the word "lexicon."

Evaluation Criteria

Each proposed term should be assessed according to four criteria. In your argumentation, address each of them.

  1. Heterogeneity of units. Does the term reflect that the set includes units of different nature: bytes, subwords, whole words, phrases, special markers? The ideal term should not be associated with only one type of unit.
  2. Statistical/encoding function. Does the term indicate that the set was formed by algorithmic/statistical methods (BPE, WordPiece, Unigram) for the purpose of encoding and text compression, and not on the basis of semantic or grammatical analysis?
  3. Communicative convenience. Is the term convenient for professional communication? Will it be understood by specialists in both linguistics and NLP? Will it cause ambiguity?
  4. Brevity and memorability. Is the term concise enough for regular use in academic texts, lectures, and discussions? Is it easy to remember and reproduce?
Recommendations for Analysis

When formulating argumentation, consider the following aspects:

Explain why your term is better suited than the existing alternatives. Provide specific examples of situations where the traditional term "token vocabulary" is misleading.

Consider how your term would be perceived in a Russian-language linguistic context and in English-language NLP publications.

Note the possible disadvantages of your term as well: no term is perfect, and recognizing limitations demonstrates the depth of analysis.
